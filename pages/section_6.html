Self Hosting vs The Cloud

In the same discourse of people wanting to regain control of what they consume in order stop being reared by large corporations, many have started to get their hands dirty by first researching alternatives and creating solutions for themselves which provide a higher degree of transparency or even full control in the case of self developed solutions.

Self-hosting for example, are websites hosted on a server that is owned and operated by the website owner. On the other hand, there is the cloud-based approach, where websites are hosted on servers that are managed by a third-party provider. This debate is closely tied to the larger discussion about the centralization vs decentralization of the internet. As people become more aware of the potential for platform capitalism to exploit their data and influence their behavior, many are seeking to regain control over their online presence by self-hosting their websites.
The holy grail of self hosting is this solar powered website. “Low-tech Magazine questions the belief in technological progress, and highlights the potential of past knowledge and technologies for designing a sustainable society. Because a web redesign was long overdue -- and because we try to practice what we preach -- we decided to build a low-tech website that meets our needs and abides by our principles. To reduce energy use, we opted for a back to basics web design, using a static site instead of a database driven content management system. We further apply default typefaces, dithered images, off-line reading options, and other tricks to lower energy use far below that of the average website. In addition, the low resource requirements and open design help to keep the blog accessible for visitors with older computers and/or less reliable Internet connections. Because it uses so little energy, this website can be run on a mini-computer with the processing power of a mobile phone. It needs 1 to 2.5 watts of power, which is supplied by a small, off-grid solar PV system on the balcony of the author’s home. Typical for off-the-grid renewable power systems, energy storage is limited. This means that the website will go off-line during longer periods of cloudy weather.”

Dissolving attention spans

The digital age has brought about numerous changes in the way individuals consume media and information. One of the most prominent of these changes is the apparent shortening of attention spans, particularly among the Z Generation.
This phenomenon can largely be attributed to two factors: the filter bubble doom scroll and the prevalence of image-based media such as TikTok. The filter bubble doom scroll refers to the personalized algorithms used by social media platforms to curate content for users, resulting in a narrow and sometimes biased view of the world. Meanwhile, TikTok, as well as other image-based media, is known for its quick-paced and highly visual content, which can be more engaging but also less informative than text-based media. In contrast, text-based media such as books or journals have been long considered a source of in-depth and thoughtful information. They offer a more comprehensive view of the world and allow for a deeper understanding of complex issues. However, the Z Generation is increasingly turning away from these sources in favor of the quick gratification and entertainment provided by image-based media. This shift has raised concerns about the potential for a decline in critical thinking skills and the spread of misinformation. As a result, it is essential to consider the impact of these media choices on the future of society and to encourage the Z Generation to engage with a more diverse range of media.

As attention spans continue to shrink, it is likely that the future of websites will be shaped by the need for quick and easily digestible content. Websites will need to be designed with a more streamlined and minimalistic approach, with a focus on visual elements rather than text-heavy content. This trend has already begun to emerge, with an increasing number of websites adopting a “card” format, where information is presented in short, bite-sized snippets.
Additionally, websites will likely incorporate more interactive and multimedia elements, such as videos and animations, to keep users engaged and to help convey information in a more engaging manner. Moreover, with the growing popularity of voice-activated virtual assistants like Siri and Alexa, websites will need to adapt to meet the needs of voice-based navigation and search. As a result, websites will likely become more conversational in nature, with a focus on natural language processing and voice-enabled interfaces.
This shift will impact both the design and development of websites, with a greater emphasis on usability and accessibility for users. The future of websites, therefore, will be shaped by the evolving needs and preferences of users, as well as the technological advancements that continue to drive change in the digital landscape.
